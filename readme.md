<h1 id="top">Single-Cell RNA-seq Analysis: Complete Workflow</h1>

<p>
This repository presents a comprehensive, end-to-end single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis workflow, integrating preprocessing, data structuring, and downstream biological analysis into a single reproducible framework. The pipeline demonstrates how raw sequencing data generated by 10X Genomics platforms can be transformed into meaningful biological insights using modern computational tools such as Galaxy, STARsolo, Scanpy, and AnnData.
</p>

<p>
At a high level, the workflow begins with raw FASTQ files and progresses through quality control, alignment, matrix generation, structured data storage, and advanced computational analysis, ultimately leading to cell-type identification and biological interpretation.
</p>


<h2>Section 1: Preprocessing (Galaxy + STARsolo)</h2>

<p>
The preprocessing stage focuses on converting raw 10X Genomics sequencing data into a clean and usable gene expression matrix. In droplet-based scRNA-seq technologies, each sequencing read contains both a cell barcode and a unique molecular identifier (UMI), which are essential for distinguishing individual cells and removing amplification biases. However, raw sequencing data also contains technical noise such as empty droplets, PCR duplicates, and low-quality reads, making preprocessing a critical step.
</p>

<p>
Using the Galaxy platform, raw FASTQ files are uploaded along with a barcode whitelist and gene annotation files. The STARsolo module, integrated within the STAR aligner, performs multiple operations in a single streamlined step, including barcode extraction, UMI handling, alignment to the reference genome (hg19), and gene quantification. This approach provides a computationally efficient alternative to traditional pipelines while maintaining high accuracy.
</p>

<p>
The output of this stage is a sparse gene expression matrix in MEX format, consisting of matrix.mtx, barcodes.tsv, and features.tsv files. Quality control metrics generated during this step indicate a high mapping efficiency of approximately 87%, reflecting reliable sequencing and alignment. Further refinement is achieved using statistical filtering methods such as DefaultDrops and EmptyDrops, which distinguish real cells from background noise. After filtering, approximately 272–279 high-quality cells are retained, forming a robust dataset suitable for downstream analysis.
</p>

<h2>Section 2: AnnData & Data Structure</h2>

<p>
Following preprocessing, the filtered gene expression matrix is organized into a structured and efficient data format using AnnData. This data structure serves as the backbone of single-cell analysis in Python, enabling seamless integration of expression data, metadata, and analytical results within a single object.
</p>

<p>
AnnData organizes the dataset into multiple components, each designed for a specific purpose. The primary expression matrix (X) stores gene expression values in a sparse format to optimize memory usage, while cell-level and gene-level annotations are stored in obs and var data frames, respectively. Additional representations of the data, such as raw counts or normalized values, are maintained in layers, allowing multiple versions of the dataset to coexist without redundancy.
</p>

<p>
Beyond basic annotations, AnnData supports multidimensional embeddings through obsm, where results from dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP are stored. Pairwise relationships between cells, such as distance matrices or neighborhood graphs, are handled through obsp, while global dataset-level metadata is preserved in the uns field. This modular yet interconnected design ensures that all aspects of the analysis remain synchronized and easily accessible.
</p>

<p>
A key advantage of AnnData is its efficient indexing and subsetting system, which allows users to filter cells and genes without disrupting the overall structure. The distinction between views and copies further enhances performance by minimizing unnecessary memory usage while still providing flexibility for independent data manipulation. Overall, AnnData provides a scalable and reproducible framework for managing complex single-cell datasets.
</p>

<h2>Section 3: Full scRNA-seq Analysis Pipeline</h2>

<p>
The final stage of the workflow involves comprehensive downstream analysis using Scanpy, transforming processed data into biologically meaningful insights. The pipeline begins with data loading and merging, ensuring that all cell and gene identifiers are unique and consistent across samples. Quality control metrics are then computed to identify potential technical artifacts, such as high mitochondrial gene expression or abnormal sequencing depth, which may indicate low-quality or stressed cells.
</p>

<p>
Subsequent filtering steps remove these unwanted cells and lowly expressed genes, improving the overall quality of the dataset. Doublet detection is performed to identify instances where two cells may have been captured together, preventing misleading hybrid expression profiles. The data is then normalized to account for differences in sequencing depth across cells, followed by log transformation to stabilize variance.
</p>

<p>
To reduce dimensionality and focus on biologically relevant signals, highly variable genes are selected, and principal component analysis (PCA) is applied. A nearest-neighbor graph is constructed to model relationships between cells, and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) is used to visualize these relationships in two-dimensional space. Clustering is performed using the Leiden algorithm, which groups cells into distinct populations based on transcriptional similarity.
</p>

<p>
The biological interpretation of these clusters is achieved through marker gene analysis, where known gene signatures are used to assign cell identities. For example, genes such as CD3D, CD3E, and CD3G are indicative of T cells, while MS4A1 marks B cells and GNLY and NKG7 identify natural killer cells. Differential expression analysis further highlights genes that distinguish each cluster, providing insights into functional differences between cell populations.
</p>

<p>
The results demonstrate successful identification of major immune cell types, including T cells, B cells, natural killer cells, and monocytes. The statistical robustness of the analysis is supported by highly significant p-values, reflecting strong biological signal and data quality. This pipeline not only validates known cellular identities but also provides a foundation for discovering novel biological patterns.
</p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>

<p>
This project delivers a complete and reproducible single-cell RNA-seq workflow that spans the entire analytical spectrum, from raw sequencing data to biological interpretation. By integrating preprocessing, structured data management, and advanced computational analysis, the pipeline ensures both accuracy and scalability.
</p>

<p>
The use of established tools and standardized methodologies makes this workflow suitable for a wide range of applications, including disease modeling, biomarker discovery, and multi-omics data integration. Its modular design allows easy adaptation to different datasets and research questions, making it a valuable resource for both beginners and advanced practitioners in the field of bioinformatics.
</p>
